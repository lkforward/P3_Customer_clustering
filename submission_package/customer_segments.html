Implementation: Outlier Detection¶

Detecting outliers in the data is extremely important in the data preprocessing step of any analysis. The presence of outliers can often skew results which take into consideration these data points. There are many "rules of thumb" for what constitutes an outlier in a dataset. Here, we will use Tukey's Method for identfying outliers: An outlier step is calculated as 1.5 times the interquartile range (IQR). A data point with a feature that is beyond an outlier step outside of the IQR for that feature is considered abnormal.

In the code block below, you will need to implement the following:

  • Assign the value of the 25th percentile for the given feature to Q1. Use np.percentile for this.
  • Assign the value of the 75th percentile for the given feature to Q3. Again, use np.percentile.
  • Assign the calculation of an outlier step for the given feature to step.
  • Optionally remove data points from the dataset by adding indices to the outliers list.

NOTE: If you choose to remove any outliers, ensure that the sample data does not contain any of these points!
Once you have performed this implementation, the dataset will be stored in the variable good_data.

Question 6¶

What are the advantages to using a K-Means clustering algorithm? What are the advantages to using a Gaussian Mixture Model clustering algorithm? Given your observations about the wholesale customer data so far, which of the two algorithms will you use and why?

Answer:

K-Means clustering algorithm is very efficient to run, but the results are sensitive to the initial position of the K centroids and can fall into local minima.

Gaussian Mixture Model (GMM) clustering assumes that all the data points are drawn from a mixture of Gaussian models (with different parameters). It uses the Expectation Maximization algorithm to find the Gaussian model which each data point is most likely to belong to. Compared to K-Means, GMM has no restriction on the structure of covariance. In other words, the distribution of data points in one cluster is more flexible (spherical, diagonal, or ties, etc) in GMM (reference 1). In addition, GMM assigns probabilities (of being in different clusters), instead of a single flag, to any data points, which gives more information. The disadvanges of GMM is when one cluster (or one Gaussian distribution) has too many data points within it, estimating the covariance matrix becomes difficult (reference 2).

Given the distribution of the wholesale data in the reduced two dimensions, the dataset is hard to separate using a simple spherical covariance. So Gaussian Mixture Model clustering is chosen for this project.

Question 10¶

Companies will often run A/B tests when making small changes to their products or services to determine whether making that change will affect its customers positively or negatively. The wholesale distributor is considering changing its delivery service from currently 5 days a week to 3 days a week. However, the distributor will only make this change in delivery service for customers that react positively. How can the wholesale distributor use the customer segments to determine which customers, if any, would react positively to the change in delivery service?
Hint: Can we assume the change affects all customers equally? How can we determine which group of customers it affects the most?

Answer:

Cluster0 has high demands for fresh food, and if the delivery service changes to 3 days a week, the quality of fresh food will be affected. Therefore, customers in Cluster0 would not like this change. Cluster1 spend most in milk, grocery and detergents_paper, and all of them are not sensitive to the delivery time. So customers in Cluster1 are more likely to respond positively.

A/B tests are a range of statistical tests used to compare two variants, A and B, in a controlled experiment. A/B tests could help the distributor to determine whether the customer satisfaction will be affected by the change of delivery schedule and to what extent.

A prerequisite for a successful A/B test is that the samples in the control group and the experiment group are expected to be drawn from the same distribution in terms of their responses to the change in test (otherwise the difference in metric won't tell the true difference caused by the change). Obviously, samples randomly drawn from the above two clusters (Cluster0 and Cluster1) will not respond to the change of delivery in the same way. Since the distributor will only make this change in delivery service for customers that react positively, we would recommend conduct an A/B tests for customers from Cluster1 only. Certain number of customers from Cluster1 can be divided into two groups (control group and experiment group), and the distributor could implement the new delivery schedule within the experiment group. Using a satisfaction index or simply the total sale as the metric, the distributor could evaluate the effect of changing delivery by checking whether the metric changed significantly in the A/B test or not. In this way, the clustering result could help improve the reliability of the A/B test.
